Field notes

What I see on the job, written down.

Twice a week — every Monday and Thursday — I publish a short, practical note for importers shipping from China to the Middle East, India and Southeast Asia. Freight rates, ports, documents, and the small things that quietly drain a margin.

—

My name is Bob Law. I am an ocean freight forwarder based in Zhongshan — one of China's largest manufacturing hubs. The two corridors I know best are China → Vietnam and China → Iraq. The wider region — the Gulf, India, Southeast Asia — I follow as a practitioner: how cargo routes, and where it tends to get stuck.

Here I write down the topics that matter most to importers: freight rates, port congestion, documentation, customs delays, and the mistakes that quietly drain your margin. I keep it practical.

Under FOB, the seller is responsible for getting goods to the named port and loading them onto the vessel. Once the cargo is on board, risk and cost transfer entirely to you, the buyer. That means you arrange the ocean freight, insurance, and all destination charges.

Here is what most people miss: as an FOB buyer, you have the right to nominate your own freight forwarder. That is a significant advantage. It means you control who handles your shipment — which affects reliability, communication, and ultimately your landed cost.

Choosing your forwarder wisely is not just a logistics decision. It is a business one.

A nominated forwarder works for you, not the factory. They pick up cargo from the supplier, handle export customs clearance, book ocean freight, and coordinate the entire journey to your port.

Why does this matter? Because when the factory selects a forwarder on your behalf, their interests and yours may not be aligned. You lose visibility, you lose cost control, and when problems arise, you are the last to know.

A trusted nominated forwarder gives you one consistent point of contact who is accountable to you at every step. That accountability is what keeps your cargo moving.

Consider the variables: multiple countries with different customs regimes, documentation requirements that vary by destination, inland routes that add significant time and cost, and geopolitical conditions that can shift rates overnight.

Then there are the ports. Jebel Ali handles enormous volume and is the regional hub, but congestion is a recurring challenge. Umm Qasr in Iraq requires careful planning around local agent networks and customs clearance timelines. Salalah, Aqaba, Dammam — each has its own rhythm.

Shipping to the Middle East is manageable when you work with someone who knows the region. It becomes expensive and unpredictable when you do not.

The impact on buyers has been significant. Transit times from China to Europe and the Middle East increased by 10 to 14 days. Freight rates spiked sharply in early 2024 and have remained elevated compared to pre-crisis levels. Equipment availability at some origin ports also tightened.

The situation continues to evolve. Some carriers have partially resumed Suez routing; others remain on Cape diversions. The result is unpredictable lead times and surcharges that can appear or disappear within weeks.

If you are planning shipments to the Middle East or Europe, build buffer time into your schedule and confirm routing with your forwarder before booking.

Mistake one: choosing purely on price. The cheapest quote rarely includes what you actually need. Low base rates are often offset by hidden surcharges and slow response times when problems arise. Mistake two: letting the factory choose. Your factory has its own preferred forwarder — and that relationship serves the factory, not you. Retain the right to nominate your own. Mistake three: not verifying route expertise. A forwarder strong on North America routes may have limited experience with Middle East customs or India port handling.

The right forwarder costs almost nothing in comparison to what the wrong one can cost you.

The main port of entry, Umm Qasr, requires careful coordination with reliable local agents who know the customs process, documentation expectations, and the handling reality on the ground. Without proper local knowledge, delays are almost certain.

Iraq also sits at an intersection of freight routing options: via Jebel Ali transshipment, via Aqaba overland, or direct calls depending on carrier and cargo type. Each option has trade-offs in cost, transit time, and risk.

This route rewards preparation and punishes improvisation. If Iraq is one of your markets, your forwarder needs to know it well — not learn it on your shipment.

Nhava Sheva is India's largest container port with extensive rail and trucking connections for national distribution. However, congestion is a persistent challenge — delays of several days are not unusual during peak periods.

Mundra, operated by Adani, has grown rapidly and now rivals Nhava Sheva in capacity. It tends to have shorter dwell times and is particularly well-suited for cargo bound for northern and western India. For buyers importing into Gujarat, Rajasthan, or Delhi NCR, Mundra can deliver faster and at lower inland cost.

The right choice depends on your destination city, your goods type, and available carrier services. Ask your forwarder to compare both options before booking.

Here is what can show up beyond the base ocean freight: origin charges (container handling, documentation, customs clearance), destination charges (port handling, examination fees, local delivery), carrier surcharges (bunker, peak season, congestion, emergency cost), and import duties, VAT, and local agent fees in your destination country.

None of this means shipping to the region is prohibitive — but your landed cost calculation needs to account for all of it. A quote that looks competitive on the base rate can end up more expensive when all charges are added.

Always ask your forwarder for an all-in breakdown before committing. Surprises at cargo arrival are the most expensive kind.

The Cape route adds approximately 10 to 14 days of sea transit time compared to the Suez route. For China to Middle East sailings, vessels that once took 20 to 25 days are now taking 30 to 40 days or more.

Freight rates have reflected this shift. Longer voyages mean higher fuel costs for carriers, passed on through surcharges. Equipment repositioning has also caused capacity tightness on some corridors.

The practical takeaway: if you are ordering from China, build extra lead time into your procurement schedule. For time-sensitive cargo, model whether air freight for part of your order makes financial sense.

For buyers importing into Singapore, Thailand, Malaysia, or Indonesia, the China-SE Asia corridor is generally well-served with competitive rates. Port Klang, Singapore, and Jakarta are major hubs with strong onward connectivity.

However, growth in volume has brought its own challenges. Port congestion at certain SE Asian hubs can extend dwell times around peak seasons. Some inland destinations require additional trucking or river logistics, adding cost and complexity.

If you are expanding sourcing into or through Southeast Asia, map your full logistics chain — not just the sea leg — before committing to suppliers.

For importers in the UAE, and for those using Dubai as a gateway to GCC markets, Jebel Ali is the default port of entry. It connects to a world-class logistics network including Jebel Ali Free Zone (JAFZA), one of the world's largest free trade zones.

For buyers in Iraq, Jordan, Saudi Arabia, and other nearby countries, cargo often passes through Jebel Ali before continuing overland or via feeder vessel. Understanding how your shipment routes through this hub helps you anticipate timing and manage costs.

Jebel Ali is efficient by regional standards — but it still experiences congestion at peak times. Plan your shipments with this in mind.

Ocean freight is cost-effective for large volumes and non-urgent cargo. A 20-foot container from China can carry thousands of units at a fraction of the air cost per unit. The trade-off is time — sea transit from China to the Middle East or India typically takes 15 to 30 days depending on routing.

Air freight is significantly more expensive per kilogram but delivers in 3 to 7 days. It makes sense for high-value goods, time-critical shipments, or small quantities where ocean freight economics do not work.

In practice, many importers use both: ocean freight for regular replenishment orders, air freight for urgent top-ups or new product samples. A good forwarder helps you model the cost difference and make the right call for each shipment.

Massive investment in infrastructure, tourism, entertainment, construction, and manufacturing is driving unprecedented demand for goods. This includes everything from industrial equipment and construction materials to consumer electronics and food products. Import volumes into the Kingdom have been growing steadily.

For logistics, this translates into increased traffic at Jeddah Islamic Port and King Abdulaziz Port in Dammam. The Saudi government has also been investing in logistics infrastructure as part of Vision 2030, aiming to position the Kingdom as a regional logistics hub.

If Saudi Arabia is one of your target markets, the opportunity is significant — but so is the competition. Reliable, compliant, on-time delivery is essential to building supplier credibility in this market.

Key fields to check: the Shipper (your factory), the Consignee (usually you or your bank), the Port of Loading, the Port of Discharge, the container number and seal number, the cargo description, and the gross weight and volume.

Common mistakes: accepting a B/L with incorrect consignee details, missing notify party information, or the wrong port of discharge — all of which can cause serious delays at customs.

Always review the draft B/L before it is issued, not after. Corrections after release can be time-consuming and expensive.

The core documents for most China export shipments: 1. Commercial Invoice — must match your PO or Letter of Credit exactly. 2. Packing List — itemizes contents, quantity, weight, and dimensions per carton. 3. Bill of Lading — the contract of carriage issued by the shipping line. 4. Certificate of Origin — may be required for preferential tariff rates. 5. Inspection Certificate — required by some buyers or countries. 6. Phytosanitary or Health Certificate — required for food or agricultural products. 7. Product compliance documents (CE, RoHS, etc.) required in your destination country.

Not every shipment needs all seven. But knowing which ones apply to your cargo — and preparing them correctly in advance — is the difference between smooth clearance and a costly hold.

Your forwarder should guide you through this for each shipment. If they do not, ask.

The most common causes: incorrect or incomplete documentation (wrong HS code, missing invoices, under-declared value), restricted or prohibited goods without proper permits, or discrepancies between what is declared and what is in the container.

How to minimize the risk: ensure all documents are correct and complete before departure. Use the correct HS code — not the closest one, the right one. If your goods require import permits or licenses in the destination country, obtain them before arrival.

A good customs broker or experienced forwarder at the destination is invaluable. Do not try to save money by skipping proper representation at customs.

FCL — Full Container Load — means your cargo occupies an entire container. You pay for the whole box regardless of fill level. FCL is faster, more secure, and typically more cost-effective when your cargo fills roughly 50% or more of a container.

LCL — Less than Container Load — means your cargo is consolidated with other shippers' goods in a shared container. You pay only for the space you use. LCL works well for small volumes, but comes with longer transit times due to consolidation and deconsolidation at each end, and slightly higher handling risk.

There is no universal right answer. It depends on your cargo volume, shipping frequency, and transit time requirements. Ask your forwarder to run the numbers for your specific situation.

First, understand what you are negotiating. Ocean freight has multiple components: the base ocean rate, origin charges, destination charges, and surcharges. The base rate is the most flexible element. Surcharges are often less negotiable because they reflect actual carrier or port costs.

What gives you leverage: volume (more TEUs = better rates), consistency (regular shipments versus spot bookings), and a long-term relationship with your forwarder. Committed volume over time is worth something tangible.

What erodes leverage: shopping rates with many different forwarders on every shipment. Forwarders prioritize clients who provide stable business. If you want better rates long-term, invest in the relationship — not just the transaction.

The five most relevant for ocean freight importers: EXW (Ex Works) — the buyer handles everything from the factory gate. FOB (Free On Board) — seller delivers to vessel; buyer takes over from loading. CFR (Cost and Freight) — seller pays ocean freight but risk passes at loading. CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) — seller pays freight and insurance. DDP (Delivered Duty Paid) — seller handles everything including import duties.

The most common terms in China export trade are FOB and CIF. FOB gives you maximum control over logistics and costs. CIF is convenient but means less visibility and control over how your cargo is handled.

My general view: for buyers with established import operations, FOB almost always delivers better value. For first-time importers who need simplicity, CIF can be a reasonable starting point — but plan to transition.

Step one: find out why. The cause matters. A vessel delay, port congestion, customs examination, or missing document each requires a different response. Your forwarder should know — and if they do not, they should be finding out.

Step two: communicate upstream and downstream. Alert your customer or warehouse if delivery will be affected. If the delay is caused by documentation issues, mobilize to fix them immediately. If it is a port delay outside your control, update your timeline and adjust accordingly.

Step three: document everything — emails, timestamps, vessel tracking screenshots. If you need to make an insurance claim or dispute storage charges later, a clear paper trail is essential.

Ocean freight involves real risk. Containers can be lost overboard, damaged by water or rough seas, or subject to theft at ports. Even if your individual shipment is safe, general average — a maritime law principle where all cargo owners share the cost of an emergency — can result in significant unexpected charges even when your cargo is undamaged.

Cargo insurance typically costs 0.1% to 0.5% of cargo value depending on goods type, routing, and coverage scope. For a $100,000 shipment, that is $100 to $500. The cost of a total loss without insurance is $100,000.

Ask your forwarder to arrange All-Risk cargo insurance for each shipment. It is not optional — it is basic risk management.

Good forwarders provide proactive tracking updates and portal access where you can monitor each milestone — cargo picked up, export customs cleared, vessel departed, transshipment completed, arrival at destination port, import customs cleared, delivered.

Beyond forwarder tools, you can track your vessel directly using free platforms like MarineTraffic or VesselFinder. For container tracking, most major carriers offer tracking on their websites using your B/L or container number.

If you regularly find yourself chasing updates from your forwarder, that is a signal. Proactive communication is a baseline standard — not a premium service.

Common surcharges you may encounter: BAF (Bunker Adjustment Factor) for fuel cost; PSS (Peak Season Surcharge) during high-demand periods; ECS (Emergency Cost Surcharge) for routing changes like current Cape diversions; GRI (General Rate Increase) applied at carrier discretion; and Destination THC (Terminal Handling Charge) at the discharge port.

The practical advice: always ask for an all-in quote that itemizes every charge. Compare quotes on total cost, not base rate. A forwarder quoting $200 base rate with $400 in surcharges is more expensive than one quoting $350 base with $150 in surcharges.

Transparency on charges is one of the easiest ways to identify a forwarder you can trust.

During Ramadan, working hours in many import markets shorten significantly. Customs clearance, port handling, and inland logistics all slow down. Port congestion often worsens as staff levels drop and pre-Ramadan stocking creates a surge of inbound cargo in the weeks before.

The practical implication: cargo that needs to arrive before Ramadan should be shipped 4 to 6 weeks ahead of the start of the holy month, depending on your routing. For time-sensitive or perishable goods, build even more buffer.

Ramadan dates shift approximately 10 to 11 days earlier each year. Mark the dates for your market well in advance and build them into your production and shipment schedule.

The India-UAE Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA), signed in 2022, significantly reduced tariffs on a wide range of goods traded between the two countries. Similar agreements are progressing with other GCC nations, creating opportunities for re-export and value-added processing.

From a logistics perspective, the India-Middle East corridor is well-served, with multiple carriers offering direct and transshipment services between Nhava Sheva, Mundra, and major Gulf ports. Transit times are typically 7 to 14 days for direct services.

For buyers who currently source exclusively from China, India is increasingly worth evaluating as a complementary source for textiles, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, steel, and engineering goods.

Key zones like Jebel Ali Free Zone (JAFZA) and Dubai Multi Commodities Centre (DMCC) allow goods to be imported, stored, processed, and re-exported without incurring UAE import duties. This makes the UAE an attractive regional distribution hub for importers serving multiple GCC or wider Middle East markets.

From a China import perspective, goods can be shipped to JAFZA, stored or light-assembled, and then distributed regionally — allowing buyers to consolidate orders from China and distribute in smaller quantities to neighboring markets.

If you are currently importing separately into each GCC country, a UAE hub-and-spoke model might significantly simplify your operations and reduce total logistics cost.

The Red Sea crisis drove Cape diversions and freight rate spikes. US-China tariff escalations have redirected sourcing flows and created complex transshipment patterns. The Russia-Ukraine conflict closed key airspace and disrupted Black Sea trade. Each of these events created ripple effects far beyond their immediate geography.

Red flag 1: They quote a very low base rate but are vague about total charges. Low base rates often conceal expensive surcharges. Red flag 2: They are slow to respond — especially when a problem arises. Speed of communication is a proxy for operational competence. Red flag 3: They have no verifiable track record on your specific route. General freight experience does not equal expertise in Iraq, India, or the Middle East.

Red flag 4: They cannot provide references from clients with similar cargo types and destinations. Red flag 5: They promise things that sound too good to be true. Unrealistically short transit times or rates far below market are either mistakes or signs of corners being cut.

Choosing a forwarder deserves at least as much due diligence as choosing a factory.

Step one: document everything immediately at the port or warehouse before moving the cargo. Photographs, videos, written description of damage. Do not accept delivery and then claim damage later — this significantly weakens your position. Step two: file a formal note of protest with the carrier or their local agent, in writing, as soon as damage is discovered. Preserve all original packaging.

Step three: notify your cargo insurance provider immediately. Every policy has a notification deadline — missing it can void your claim. Step four: obtain an independent survey report from a licensed cargo surveyor if the damage is significant. This professional assessment is essential documentation for an insurance or carrier liability claim.

Most damage claims are recoverable — if you follow the right steps in the right order. The biggest mistakes are delay, lack of documentation, and not having insurance to begin with.

Chinese National Golden Week falls in early October. Factories typically close for 7 to 10 days. The weeks before are a rush to complete orders and clear cargo, creating port congestion and equipment shortages. If your cargo needs to be in-market before November, book and ship by mid-September.

The pre-Christmas peak runs from approximately August to October, as buyers stock warehouses for Q4 demand. Freight rates during this period are almost always higher than the rest of the year, and space can be tight on popular routes.

The solution: plan your orders earlier than you think necessary, book space in advance, and communicate clearly with your forwarder about your target arrival dates.

Step two: provide requested documents immediately. Every day of delay generates storage and detention charges at the port or customs facility. If customs needs additional invoices, certificates, or permits, prioritize getting them across quickly.

Step three: if the hold is due to goods classified as restricted or requiring a license, engage a licensed customs consultant immediately. Prevention is better than cure: ensure all documentation is correct before departure, and work with a forwarder who knows the customs requirements of your specific destination country.

A delayed shipment in peak season can mean missing your sales window, paying air freight to urgently replenish, or facing customer penalties. A documentation error can result in customs fines, storage charges of $100 to $300 per day per container, and re-inspection fees. A forwarder who low-balls their quote can leave you with an unexpected invoice at cargo release.

Beyond direct financial costs, there is the operational cost of your time: hours spent chasing updates, correcting documents, resolving disputes, managing disappointed customers. This is time you could spend growing your business.

The freight forwarder you choose is not a commodity vendor. They are a critical operational partner. The cheapest option on paper is rarely the cheapest option in reality.

Landed cost = Factory price + International freight + Cargo insurance + Origin charges + Destination charges + Import duties and taxes + Local delivery to warehouse.

Example for a $10,000 FOB order from China to UAE: Ocean freight (20-foot FCL) approximately $1,200-$2,000 depending on season. Insurance approximately $30-$80. Origin charges approximately $200-$350. Destination charges approximately $200-$400. UAE import duty varies by HS code. UAE VAT at 5% applies to most goods.

Total landed cost for this example is typically 25-50% above the factory price. Always calculate this before setting your selling price or evaluating a supplier quote. Your margin lives in the gap between landed cost and selling price.

Before CNY: place orders early — ideally 6 to 8 weeks before the holiday if you need goods before the break. Book freight space early — capacity fills up fast. Confirm production completion dates with your factory in writing.

During CNY: most factories and many service providers are on limited staff or fully closed. Do not expect standard responsiveness. Have your critical shipments moving before the holiday starts.

After CNY: expect a surge. Factories restart and release accumulated cargo simultaneously, creating congestion at ports and tightening equipment availability for 2 to 4 weeks. If you can shift non-urgent shipments to March, that window is often more comfortable. CNY disruption is predictable — which means it is entirely manageable with advance planning.

Your cargo shares a container with other shippers' goods. If another shipment in the same container has a problem — customs hold, incorrect documentation, prohibited goods — your cargo may be delayed even though your own documentation is perfect. This is a systemic risk you absorb simply by being in a shared container.

There is also a handling risk. LCL cargo is loaded and unloaded at least twice — at the origin consolidation warehouse and at the destination deconsolidation facility. More handling means more opportunity for damage, especially for fragile goods.

LCL still makes excellent sense for many shipments. But go in with eyes open about co-loading risk, and make sure your cargo insurance covers LCL handling specifically.

Congestion is typically driven by volume surges, vessel bunching after Red Sea or Cape routing changes, labor constraints, or weather events. During these periods, container dwell times can extend from the standard 5 to 7 days to 2 to 3 weeks or more.

What to do if your cargo is affected: get regular updates from your destination agent on actual yard status. Document storage charges carefully — in some cases carrier liability or force majeure provisions may apply. If your goods are time-critical, ask your agent whether any priority handling options are available.

Most importantly: when congestion is anticipated — you can often see this coming in industry updates — adjust your order timing or shipping schedule proactively. Being reactive to port congestion is always more expensive than anticipating it.

Spot rate hunting does work in high-supply, low-demand periods when carrier capacity is plentiful and rates are soft. But when capacity tightens, buyers with no established carrier or forwarder relationships are the last to get space — or they pay a premium for it.

What long-term partnerships deliver: consistent service, priority space allocation in tight markets, better problem-solving when things go wrong, and a forwarder who understands your cargo, your customers, and your priorities.

My recommendation: negotiate competitive rates with a primary forwarder based on committed volume. Keep a backup option. Stop treating every shipment as a zero-sum price negotiation.
