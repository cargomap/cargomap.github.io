China → Gulf & Middle East

What I know about this corridor, written from where I actually stand — and honest about where my hands-on experience ends.

Where I stand

I know the Gulf first as a transshipment region — because my Iraq cargo passes through it.

My deepest lane into the Middle East is China → Iraq, and in today's conditions almost everything on it transships through a Gulf hub — Khor Fakkan, Sohar or Fujairah — before the final leg to Umm Qasr. So the part of the Gulf I watch most closely is the part where shipments actually get stuck: the transfer between the mainline vessel and the feeder. The wider Gulf — the UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman and Jordan — I know as a practitioner: the gateways, how cargo routes, and the documentation that tends to hold goods at the border. On this page I keep those two things separate, so you always know what comes from my own shipments and what comes from general experience.

The routing reality

Gulf-bound cargo skips the Red Sea — but the region isn't risk-free right now.

From South China, cargo for the Arabian Gulf runs east through the Malacca Strait and across the Indian Ocean to the Strait of Hormuz. It does not pass through the Red Sea or the Suez Canal, so the Houthi attacks that have kept most carriers on the long Cape of Good Hope routing for Europe and the US East Coast largely don't touch Jebel Ali, Dammam or the other Gulf ports. As of mid-2026 that Red Sea disruption is still active and tied to the wider regional conflict — attacks resumed after the ceasefire broke down earlier this year, and the industry isn't expecting a return to the Suez route before 2027. Jeddah, on Saudi Arabia's Red Sea coast, is the one Gulf-region port that does share that exposure.

The chokepoint that actually matters for Gulf-bound cargo is the Strait of Hormuz, which every vessel calling at Jebel Ali, Dammam or the other Gulf ports must pass through. It hasn't closed, and neighbouring governments have been vocal about keeping it open, but regional tension has kept it a live topic. I haven't seen it disrupt schedules on my shipments so far — but it's the risk I actually watch on this corridor, not the Red Sea.

The ports

The gateways and hubs across the corridor.

01

Jebel Ali, Dubai (UAE)

Regional hub

The Gulf's largest port and the default gateway for the UAE and much of the GCC. Alongside it sits the JAFZA free zone, which is useful for storage, consolidation and re-export when you serve several regional markets from one base.

02

Khor Fakkan · Fujairah · Sohar

Transship hubs I route through

On the Gulf of Oman, outside the Strait of Hormuz. These are the hubs where my China → Iraq cargo transfers from a mainline vessel to the feeder for the final leg. The transshipment step is exactly where shipments stall, so it is where I pay the most attention and tell you the truth about timing.

03

King Abdulaziz Port, Dammam (Saudi Arabia)

Gateway · general knowledge

Saudi Arabia's main Arabian Gulf gateway, serving the industrial Eastern Province and the inland route to Riyadh. For eastern and central Saudi destinations it is usually the shorter inland option.

04

Jeddah Islamic Port (Saudi Arabia)

Gateway · Red Sea exposure

The Kingdom's largest container port, on the Red Sea. Unlike the Arabian Gulf ports, Jeddah-bound cargo is exposed to Red Sea routing conditions, so I factor that into timing for the western region, Makkah and Madinah.

05

Salalah (Oman) & the smaller Gulf states

Gateways · general knowledge

Salalah is a major Arabian Sea transshipment port. Hamad Port (Qatar), Shuwaikh and Shuaiba (Kuwait) and Khalifa Bin Salman (Bahrain) are the main gateways for the smaller states, most of them fed through the larger regional hubs.

06

Umm Qasr (Iraq) & Aqaba (Jordan)

Iraq is the lane I know best

Umm Qasr is covered in depth on its own page — see China → Iraq. Aqaba, Jordan's only seaport, also serves as an overland bridge into the Levant and toward Iraq.

Documents & conformity

In this region, the paperwork decides the timing.

The recurring lesson across the Gulf is that conformity is arranged before the cargo ships, not after it arrives. Saudi Arabia runs its SABER / SALEEM system, where regulated products need a Product Certificate of Conformity and a per-shipment certificate issued before arrival. The UAE has its own conformity requirements for regulated categories. Iraq requires a Certificate of Conformity for many goods — the single most common cause of delay on that lane. The detail varies by country and by product, so the practical step is always the same: confirm whether your specific goods need certification for your specific destination, and arrange it at origin. Get that right and clearance across the region is workable; leave it to chance and your container waits while charges build.

Common questions

About this corridor.

Does the Red Sea situation affect shipping from China to the Gulf?

Only indirectly. Gulf ports like Jebel Ali and Dammam route via the Indian Ocean and the Strait of Hormuz, not the Red Sea and Suez, so the Houthi attacks that have most carriers avoiding Suez don't reroute this cargo. That disruption is still active as of mid-2026 and isn't expected to ease before 2027. Jeddah, on Saudi Arabia's Red Sea coast, is the one Gulf-region port that does share that exposure.

Is the Strait of Hormuz a risk for shipments to the Gulf?

It's the one chokepoint every Gulf-bound vessel from China has to pass, so it's worth watching given current regional tension. I haven't seen it disrupt schedules on my shipments, and neighbouring governments have said they intend to keep it open, but I'd rather flag it honestly than suggest the wider region is risk-free.

How much of the Gulf have you handled yourself?

My firsthand experience is the transshipment hubs — Khor Fakkan, Sohar and Fujairah — because my China → Iraq cargo routes through them. The wider Gulf ports I know as a practitioner rather than from shipping into each one every week, and I'll always tell you plainly which is which.

What usually holds cargo up at a Gulf border?

Most often it is conformity certification or a documentation mismatch — the right certificate not arranged before shipment, or the invoice, packing list and goods not lining up. These are preventable at origin, which is where I'd rather solve them.
